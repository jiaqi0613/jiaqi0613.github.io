Catalog Navigation and Discovery

The product was growing. The navigation wasn't keeping up.

How do you help users find the right product in a growing, multi-category catalog, when they don't yet know what they're looking for? Five major iterations, driven by user research and quantitative data, toward a taxonomy that teaches as it guides.

Overview

The same challenge in every growing product: structure that doesn't scale

Dossier is a DTC fragrance brand offering 150+ perfumes across two distinct lines, Dossier Impressions (designer-inspired) and Dossier Originals (in-house), plus Gift Sets and Home Scents. As the catalog expanded, the navigation became a barrier to discovery.

I owned the catalog experience end-to-end as part of a 3-person design team: user research, flows, prototypes, high-fidelity visuals, and QC across six international storefronts (US, UK, Canada, Australia, Mexico, EU), including high-stakes launches and sales events like Black Friday. Every component fed into our living design system, maintained with the visual design team to ensure brand consistency across the product.

The Problem

The taxonomy was organized for the business. Not for the user.

The catalog was structured around internal logic of product lines, gender, and inventory groups, but users think in intentions and questions: "What's the difference between Impressions and Originals? Do they sell home fragrance?" CRO data confirmed high drop-off, and user sessions showed hesitation before any browsing began.

Mismatched mental models: Breadcrumbs were structured by gender. The catalog grouped by product type. Two different organizing principles running in parallel, neither matching how users naturally thought about fragrance.
Invisible product lines: "Dossier Impressions" and "Dossier Originals" were listed as labels, not explained. Users skipped past the distinction entirely, reducing two meaningfully different offerings to a single undifferentiated list.
Missing categories & misleading labels: Home Scents sat entirely outside the main navigation, but when a 'Home' label did appear, user interviews revealed a consistent misread: users assumed it linked back to the homepage, not a product category. A labeling problem was quietly compounding a visibility problem.
No shared language: Users thought in scents like "something warm," "fresh and citrusy," but the catalog spoke in brand names and product lines. The system and the user were speaking different languages.

Research and Discovery

Quantitative data showed where users dropped. Research showed why.

CRO data (heatmaps, funnels, scroll depth) told us where the system was failing. Moderated and unmoderated user interviews told us why, surfacing mental models we hadn't anticipated and giving us an evidence-backed foundation for every design decision.

"Users weren't browsing, they were trying to orient. They needed to understand what Dossier offered and where they fit within it before they could choose anything."

A second signal from CRO: many users arrived already knowing a designer fragrance they loved, and were looking for an accessible alternative, not exploring. This gave us two distinct user types, the explorer and the seeker, that became the framework driving every subsequent iteration.

Design Evolution: From 1.0 to 4.0

Five iterations. Each one shaped by what the previous version taught us.

v1.0, The Starting Point

State: functional but not designed to scale

The original catalog was flat and transactional: five tabs (All, Dossier Impressions, Dossier Originals, Sets, Bestsellers) with gender pills as the primary filter. Home Scents didn't exist as a category yet. There was no hierarchy, no explanatory framing, and no path for users who didn't already know what they were looking for.

This version worked at a small scale. But as the product library grew, it couldn't hold the weight of a more complex offering. The taxonomy was organized for the business, not for the user's mental model.

v2.0, First Structural Attempt

Key decision: expand the taxonomy, introduce brand context

As the product line expanded, we took a first pass at restructuring. A hero image gave the page a stronger brand entry point. We brought Home Scents into the main navigation for the first time, solving the invisible category problem. Discovery Set, a curated product rather than a category, also gained dedicated visibility here. Sort and filter controls gave users more agency.

But user research revealed the core problem persisted: users still moved through the tabs without understanding what "Dossier Impressions" or "Dossier Originals" meant. Labels alone don't build mental models. We needed framing, not just structure.

v2.5, Intent-Led Discovery

Key decision: meet the seeker where they are

CRO data showed a clear behavioral pattern: many users arrived already knowing a designer fragrance, Santal 33, Baccarat Rouge 540, Black Opium, and wanted to find the Dossier version. They had intent. The catalog wasn't capturing it.

We introduced brand shortcut pills directly in the search bar, Tom Ford, YSL, Chanel, Creed, La Labo, Dior, Jo Malone, By Kilian, letting seekers navigate immediately to inspired-by alternatives. This was a shift from passive browsing to intent-led discovery, and it reinforced Dossier's core value proposition (designer quality, accessible price) within the navigation itself.

User research had also surfaced a distinct gifting persona, customers shopping for others rather than themselves, with different intent and different decision criteria. Timed deliberately ahead of Black Friday, we introduced Gift Sets as a dedicated navigation tab, giving this persona a direct entry point at exactly the right moment in the purchase calendar. This was persona-driven design meeting commercial timing.

v3.0, Elevated Visual Hierarchy (Current State)

Key decision: make categories feel meaningful, then course-correct

Version 3.0 was the most visually ambitious. We replaced the text tab bar with thumbnail category tiles, each category (All Perfumes, Impressions, Originals, Gift Sets, Home Scents, Bestsellers, New Arrivals) paired with a visual image. The intent was to make categories feel meaningful rather than administrative, and give the page a more editorial, premium feel. We also elevated the filter system to include Scent Family and Collections as first-class options.

The ambition was right. The execution revealed a trade-off: the tiles consumed significant page real estate before users reached products. Behavioral data flagged clear hesitation at this stage. The visual hierarchy was serving the brand more than the user, prioritizing aesthetics over speed of access.

This version was valuable precisely because it failed instructively. It clarified a principle that shaped everything after: in a discovery-oriented interface, the fastest path to the item wins.

v4.0, Clarity Over Decoration (Coming soon)

Key decision: remove complexity, add shared language

Version 4.0 applied the lesson from 3.0 directly. We stripped the visual tiles and returned to a clean, scannable text tab bar (All Perfumes, Impressions, Originals, Gift Sets, Home Scents, Bestsellers, New Arrivals), preserving the full taxonomy we'd built without the friction overhead. The guiding principle: simple, ergonomic interfaces win over visually rich ones when speed of access is what users need most.

The decisive addition: scent family tags on every product card (Earthy, Warm, Flowery, Gourmand, Fresh, Herbal). These create a shared language between the user and the catalog. Users who don't know brand names or product lines can now navigate by what they actually know: how they want to smell.

The filter bar (Gender, Scent Family, Inspired-by Brands, Collections) is now persistent and coherent with the card-level taxonomy. For the first time, every layer of the system speaks the same language, a quality bar I held throughout: every component needed to be both purposeful and precise.

What's Next, Proactive Design Direction

Shop by Scent Family: completing the discovery system

Scent family tags on cards are useful, but they're not yet a first-class navigation layer. I've proposed surfacing scent families as a primary entry point in both the catalog and main menu, letting users discover by preference rather than category. It serves both user types: explorers get a clear starting point; seekers get a faster path to what they actually want.

Outcome

A scalable information architecture, and a team that ships with evidence

Five iterations built a research-and-design loop that got faster and more confident each time. The taxonomy today is built to scale, so new categories and product lines can be added without restructuring the whole. The broader signal: Dossier was acquired by American Pacific Group (APG), delivering a 50x return for lead investor Otium, a rare outcome in DTC. The catalog is the brand's front door. Making it work at scale was part of making that growth sustainable.

Reflection

The hardest part of designing complex systems is knowing what to remove

Version 3.0 was visually ambitious, strategically coherent, and behaviorally wrong. The data didn't lie. It taught me that the goal isn't to represent all the complexity, it's to build a system the user can hold in their head.

The principle I kept returning to: good ergonomics means the interface disappears. When navigation works, users don't notice it, they're just finding what they need. That's the bar I held across every iteration. And it's exactly the standard I want to bring to tools built for creators like designers, architects, and builders who live inside an interface all day.
